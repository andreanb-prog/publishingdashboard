While you wait — a quick guide
The number that matters most
CTR (link click-through rate) is your report card.
CTR is the percentage of people who saw your ad and clicked the link to your book. It tells you whether your hook, image, and copy are connecting with your target readers. Everything else is secondary until this number is healthy.
Under 5%
Something is wrong with the creative. Fix the hook or image before spending more.
5–10%
You're building something. Look at your copy — a small tweak might push it over.
10–15%
On track. Keep running. Watch it a few more days before scaling.
15%+
This is the target. Your hook is landing. Hold and consider increasing your budget.
Why romance and mystery ads work differently
Your readers buy tropes, not summaries.
Romance and mystery readers know exactly what emotional experience they're looking for before they click. An ad that leads with the trope — "forbidden romance," "mafia protector," "grumpy/sunshine" — will almost always outperform one that describes the plot. Your hook is doing the heaviest lifting. If your CTR is low, the first thing to look at is whether your first line signals the trope clearly and fast.
What "still learning" means
Under 500 impressions? Don't touch it.
When you first launch, Meta is in a learning phase — testing your ad against different slices of your audience to find readers most likely to click. Day-one numbers almost always look rough. They almost always improve by day three. Any campaign under 500 impressions in your results today is not ready to judge — this tool will tell you that clearly.
On naming your campaigns
Good names unlock better insights over time.
If your campaigns are named "Campaign 1" or "August Ad," it's nearly impossible to learn anything across months of data. When you include the trope, hook, and audience in the name, patterns emerge: "Forbidden romance hooks outperform grumpy/sunshine 2:1 with BookTok audiences." If your CTR is under 10%, the creative is usually the culprit. Here's what makes romance and mystery readers stop scrolling.

Don't do this
"A story of love, betrayal, and second chances."
Tells the reader nothing. Every romance is a story of love and something. No trope, no hook, no reason to click.
Do this instead — enemies to lovers / protector
"He's her enemy. He's her boss. And he's the only one keeping her alive."
Signals enemies-to-lovers and protector romance in three lines. The reader knows immediately if this is for her.
Mafia / dark romance
"He doesn't ask. He takes. And she's next."
Direct, dangerous, on-genre. Dark romance readers know exactly what this is.
The fastest diagnostic: read your hook out loud. Does it tell your reader the trope in the first line? If not, that's the rewrite.
